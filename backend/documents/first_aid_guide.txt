OFFICIAL RED CROSS TRAUMA FIRST AID GUIDE

SEVERE BLEEDING TREATMENT
Severe uncontrolled bleeding is a primary cause of shock and death in trauma scenarios. Follow these steps immediately:
1. Apply firm, direct pressure: Place a clean cloth, sterile gauze, or bandage directly over the bleeding wound. Press down firmly with both hands.
2. Maintain constant pressure: Do not lift the bandage to check if bleeding has stopped, as this disrupts early blood clot formation. Keep pressing constantly for a minimum of 5 to 10 minutes.
3. Elevate the injury: If the wound is on a limb (arm or leg), raise the limb above the level of the heart while maintaining pressure to reduce gravitational blood pressure at the cut site.
4. Apply pressure dressings: Wrap a secondary bandage tightly over the primary cloth to secure pressure. If blood leaks through the bandage, do not remove it. Wrap an additional thick layer of cloth on top and keep pressing.
5. Tourniquet application: For extreme arterial limb bleeds where pressure fails, tie a thick cloth strip 2 inches above the wound and twist it with a stick until the bleeding stops. Write down the exact time of tourniquet application.

CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION (CPR)
CPR is a life-saving procedure performed when a person's breathing or heartbeat has stopped.
1. Verify responsiveness: Shake the person's shoulders and ask loudly, "Are you okay?". Check for normal chest rise or breathing.
2. Position the victim: Place the person flat on their back on a firm, hard surface.
3. Hand placement: Place the heel of one hand in the absolute center of the chest (lower half of the breastbone). Place your other hand directly on top of the first, interlocking your fingers.
4. Perform chest compressions: Lean over the victim with your elbows locked straight. Compress the chest hard and fast. The depth must be at least 2 inches (5 cm).
5. Compression rate: Maintain a rapid tempo of 100 to 120 compressions per minute (e.g., to the rhythm of the song "Staying Alive"). Allow the chest to rise completely between compressions.
6. Cycle: Continue compressions continuously. If trained, perform a ratio of 30 compressions followed by 2 quick rescue breaths. Otherwise, perform hands-only CPR continuously until medical help arrives.

FRACTURE AND SPLINT STABILIZATION
For suspected bone breaks or joint dislocations:
1. Do not move the broken bone: Never attempt to force a bone back into alignment or push protruding bone fragments back beneath the skin.
2. Immobilize the injury: Wrap a firm board, long stick, or rolled magazine along the joints above and below the fracture site. Secure the splint with cloth ties.
3. Check pulse: Ensure that splint ties are not wrapped too tight by checking that the fingers or toes remain warm and maintain a pulse.

SNAKE BITE EMERGENCY
If bitten by a venomous snake:
1. Keep perfectly still: Moving around increases blood circulation, which spreads the venom rapidly through the body.
2. Keep the bite level below the heart: This slows down the spread of venom into the chest.
3. Do NOT cut the wound: Do not attempt to cut the skin or suck out the venom, as this is ineffective and causes severe tissue infections.
4. Clean and bandage: Clean the bite area with clean water, wrap a wide bandage moderately tight along the entire limb, and wait for emergency transport. Do not apply ice.
